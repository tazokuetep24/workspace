\begin{abstract}
Large generative models provide strong capabilities in natural-language processing,
code generation, and reasoning, but their inference requires substantial computation,
memory capacity, memory bandwidth, and energy. These requirements present a particular
challenge for deployment on resource-constrained edge devices.

This paper surveys resource-efficient inference in decoder-based large language models.
It first outlines the relevant components of the Transformer architecture and examines
the two principal stages of inference: the parallel prefill phase and the sequential
autoregressive decode phase. This analysis identifies the main resource bottlenecks,
including the computational and memory cost of attention, repeated model-weight
transfers, and the growth of the Key--Value cache.

Based on these bottlenecks, the paper reviews optimisation methods at several levels of
the inference stack. These include model compression through quantisation, pruning, and
knowledge distillation; optimised attention computation and Key--Value cache management;
decoding and scheduling methods such as speculative decoding and continuous batching;
and system-level runtimes including \texttt{llama.cpp}, MLC-LLM, and vLLM. The methods
are compared according to the resources they reduce, their accuracy and performance
trade-offs, and their suitability for different deployment environments.

The central finding is that resource-efficient inference cannot be achieved through a
single universally optimal method. Effective deployment requires identifying the dominant
bottleneck of the target platform and combining compatible model-level, algorithmic,
decoding, and system-level optimisations accordingly.
\end{abstract}
